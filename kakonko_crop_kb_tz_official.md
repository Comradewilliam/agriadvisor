# Kakonko District KB Draft: Tanzania Official Guidance
**Scope:** Maize, common beans (maharage), groundnuts (karanga), and mawese/michikichi (oil palm).  
**Use case:** RAG / knowledge base ingestion for district-level agronomy guidance.

**How to read this file:**  
Each row follows:

`Crop → Growth stage → Problem/Situation → Solution/Action`

**Important note on scope:** This is a synthesis of the main Tanzania official / official-parastatal sources I could verify quickly (MoA, TARI, and TARI-linked research and crop guidance). It is broad and practical, not a legal or regulatory compendium.  
**Note on terminology:** I treated **“mawese”** as **michikichi / oil palm** because that is how Tanzanian official crop guidance commonly frames the crop.

## Maize (mahindi)

| Crop | Growth stage | Problem / Situation | Solution / Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Maize | Land preparation | Soil not ready before the first rains; hard soil after heavy rain; poor drainage or waterlogging. | Prepare the field early before the first rains. Plough about 15–20 cm deep. Use ridges / mounds in places that hold water. A non-selective herbicide can also be used instead of ploughing where appropriate. |
| Maize | Variety / seed selection | Wrong variety for the season, altitude, rainfall, or farming objective. | Choose the variety based on the goal of production, season (masika / vuli), altitude, and rainfall. Hybrid seed is used for one season; OPV seed can be reused for 2–3 seasons. |
| Maize | Planting | Late planting increases risk, especially for streak / milia-prone varieties. | Plant early. For varieties sensitive to maize streak virus, delayed planting increases damage. Use the spacing recommended for the specific variety and zone. |
| Maize | Plant population | Too few or too many plants, poor stand establishment. | Target the recommended stand: about 40,000–50,000 plants/ha in general, and 60,000–70,000 plants/ha for short, early-maturing varieties like Kito and Katumani. Thin excess plants 2–3 weeks after emergence. |
| Maize | Planting arrangement | Poor spacing reduces yield. | For long-duration varieties, use about 75 × 30 cm or 90 × 25 cm with one plant per hole. In dry areas, wider spacing such as 100 × 50 cm with two plants per hole is recommended. |
| Maize | Nutrient management | Low soil fertility, weak early growth, low yields. | Apply farmyard manure or compost before planting. The maize guide gives about 10–15 tonnes/ha of manure (4–6 tonnes/acre). Fertilizer works best when placed close to the seed but not touching it. |
| Maize | Nutrient management | Nitrogen and phosphorus shortages, especially in low-rainfall and coastal areas. | Split fertilizer: apply about 30–50% at planting and the rest when maize reaches about 1 m height or earlier depending on crop condition. Phosphorus should be applied fully at planting. |
| Maize | Intercropping / soil health | Weed pressure, erosion, and pest damage. | Intercrop maize with legumes such as beans, cowpea, groundnut, or pigeonpea. This improves soil fertility, reduces weeds and erosion, and can reduce pest pressure. |
| Maize | Early vegetative stage | Weeds compete for water, nutrients, and light. | Keep the field weed-free during the first 40 days after emergence. Two hand weedings are usually enough in low, middle, and highland zones, with timing adjusted by region. |
| Maize | Vegetative to reproductive stage | Five major leaf diseases reduce yield: two rusts, two leaf blights, and viral streak disease. | The maize guide says these diseases are not profitably controlled with chemicals. Use tolerant or resistant varieties as the main control measure. |
| Maize | Vegetative to reproductive stage | Maize streak disease can stop normal cob formation if infection happens early. | Plant early and use resistant / tolerant varieties. Avoid relying on insecticide sprays as the main solution for the disease vector. |
| Maize | Grain / cob stage | Ear and kernel diseases such as Gibberella, Fusarium, and Diplodia attack the cob and seed, especially in wet areas. | Remove and destroy or bury infected plants and cobs. Keep the crop healthy, avoid mechanical damage, and reduce field moisture stress where possible. |
| Maize | Harvest maturity | Harvesting too early reduces grain fill and yield; too late increases losses. | Harvest about 7–8 weeks after flowering when physiological maturity is reached. Signs include yellowing leaves, husks turning pale yellow, and the black layer on kernels. |
| Maize | Post-harvest drying and storage | Weevils, larger grain borer, and other store pests damage grain. | Dry grain well before storage. Use clean stores, airtight containers / bins where possible, or approved grain protectants. The maize guide cites Actellic / Malathion options for stored grain protection. |
| Maize | Post-harvest store hygiene | Old grain residues and dirty stores raise infestation risk. | Clean the store before the new crop is put inside. Keep the store dry and sanitary at least once a year before new grain is stored. |

## Common beans (maharage)

| Crop | Growth stage | Problem / Situation | Solution / Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Common beans | Land preparation | Poorly prepared field, low aeration, and poor root development. | Prepare a fine, well-aerated seedbed before the rains. Use a well-drained field and avoid waterlogging. |
| Common beans | Site selection | Beans are sensitive to poor drainage and low soil fertility. | Choose a field with good drainage and fertile soil, ideally around pH 5–7. Avoid places where water stands after rainfall. |
| Common beans | Seed selection | Local seed is often low-yielding and may not resist disease well. | Use improved, certified seed from recognized sources. Prefer disease-resistant varieties and seed that is clean and true-to-type. |
| Common beans | Planting / basal nutrition | Low fertility limits growth and pod formation. | The official bean guide snippet indicates basal fertilizer options at planting such as TSP with CAN, or Urea, and also DAP / Minjingu Mazao under recommendation. Use the locally recommended package after soil advice. |
| Common beans | Planting | Planting when soil moisture is insufficient causes poor emergence. | Plant when there is enough moisture in the soil. Do not plant into very dry soil. |
| Common beans | Emergence / early growth | Young plants can be attacked by leaf-feeding pests and stem pests. | Monitor early and protect the stand quickly when damage appears. The TARI bean pest material specifically educates farmers on Ootheca (bean leaf beetles) and other bean pests. |
| Common beans | Early vegetative stage | Weeds reduce light, water, and nutrients. | Weed early and keep the field clean during the critical early growth period. |
| Common beans | Vegetative to flowering stage | Anthracnose, angular leaf spot, bean common mosaic virus (BCMV), common bacterial blight, and leaf rust reduce bean yield across Tanzania. | Use resistant varieties where available, plant clean seed, and remove heavily infected plants. Keep the field clean and avoid moving disease with infected seed or crop residues. |
| Common beans | Vegetative to flowering stage | Seedborne and residue-borne diseases spread quickly in repeated bean fields. | Rotate crops and do not keep beans in the same field season after season. Use field sanitation and healthy seed. |
| Common beans | Flowering / pod set | Stress at flowering reduces pod numbers and pod filling. | Avoid moisture stress, heavy weed pressure, and disease outbreaks at flowering. Maintain timely field scouting and prompt control measures. |
| Common beans | Pod filling | Pod and seed filling can be reduced by pests, disease, and drought stress. | Protect the crop from stress until pods mature. Keep the crop healthy and manage water where irrigation is possible. |
| Common beans | Maturity / harvesting | Delayed harvesting leads to pod shattering and field losses. | Harvest promptly when most pods are mature and dry. Avoid leaving mature pods too long in the field. |
| Common beans | Post-harvest drying | Grain stored too wet spoils and loses quality. | Dry beans thoroughly before storage. Keep them under cover, away from rain and ground moisture. |
| Common beans | Post-harvest storage | Insects and fungal spoilage reduce grain quality and seed viability. | Store only clean, dry grain in clean containers or bags. Keep the store dry and inspect regularly. |

## Groundnuts / karanga

| Crop | Growth stage | Problem / Situation | Solution / Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Groundnuts | Land preparation | Waterlogging, compacted soil, or poor root development. | Use well-drained land. The official Tanzania groundnut material says groundnuts perform best on suitable soils below 1500 m altitude and in areas with appropriate rainfall and soil conditions. |
| Groundnuts | Seed selection | Improved seed is not always available; old varieties can be low-yielding. | Use improved varieties and quality seed. TARI-linked studies name varieties such as Pendo 1998, Johari 1985, Naliendele 2009, Mnanje 2009, Mangaka 2009, and Nachi 2015 as key farmer-used improved varieties. |
| Groundnuts | Planting | Late planting or poor seed quality lowers stand establishment. | Plant at the onset of rains using clean, viable seed. Use local agronomist guidance on spacing and seed rate for the variety. |
| Groundnuts | Early establishment | Poor emergence and weak seedlings reduce final yield. | Use quality seed and a fine seedbed. Keep the field free from standing water and crusting. |
| Groundnuts | Vegetative stage | Weeds compete strongly in the early crop period. | Weed early and keep the crop clean. Groundnut performance drops sharply when weeds are not controlled in time. |
| Groundnuts | Vegetative to flowering stage | Drought stress is one of the major production constraints in Tanzania. | Use moisture-conserving practices, plant at the right time, and choose drought-tolerant improved varieties where available. |
| Groundnuts | Flowering / pegging / pod formation | Groundnut rosette disease is most devastating under rainfall conditions; rust thrives under high humidity and high temperature; early and late leaf spot also reduce yield. | Use resistant or tolerant varieties, scout early, and apply integrated disease management. The official variety catalogue identifies rosette, rust, and leaf spot as major threats. |
| Groundnuts | Flowering / pegging / pod formation | Pest and disease pressure can cause major yield loss and lower quality. | Keep fields monitored and use recommended crop protection measures from extension services. Avoid practices that increase stress at critical growth stages. |
| Groundnuts | Pod filling | Aflatoxin risk rises when pods are stressed, damaged, or exposed to moisture and poor drying. | Harvest on time, minimize pod damage, and dry pods quickly and properly. Keep moisture low to limit fungal contamination. |
| Groundnuts | Maturity / harvesting | Over-maturity can increase pod loss and quality deterioration. | Harvest when pods are mature. Do not leave mature pods too long in the soil. |
| Groundnuts | Drying | High moisture in pods or grain causes mold and aflatoxin risk. | Dry pods and grain thoroughly before storage. Use clean drying surfaces and protect from rain. |
| Groundnuts | Storage | Stored groundnuts are vulnerable to mold and aflatoxin contamination. | Store only dry, clean groundnuts in a dry, ventilated place. Avoid damaged pods and keep the store clean. |

## Mawese / michikichi (oil palm)

| Crop | Growth stage | Problem / Situation | Solution / Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mawese / oil palm | Site and planting material selection | Poor-quality planting material lowers yield and oil quality. | Use verified, high-quality planting material. TARI Kihinga explicitly highlights good planting material for higher yield, better disease / pest tolerance, and improved palm oil quality. |
| Mawese / oil palm | Seed preparation | The seed has a hard shell that prevents rapid water uptake and delays germination. | Follow the official TARI seed preparation and germination guide for michikichi, because the shell must be managed so the seed can absorb water and sprout. |
| Mawese / oil palm | Nursery stage | Weak or uneven seedlings reduce field performance. | Raise healthy seedlings in the nursery and select only vigorous seedlings for transplanting. |
| Mawese / oil palm | Transplanting | Poor transplanting reduces survival and future bunch yield. | Transplant only strong, healthy nursery seedlings and establish them in a suitable field following the local TARI / extension protocol. |
| Mawese / oil palm | Early field establishment | Weed pressure and competition slow growth. | Weed regularly and keep young palms well maintained. |
| Mawese / oil palm | Vegetative growth | Low vigor and poor nutrition reduce later bunch production. | Maintain the stand well, keep it weed-free, and follow local soil-fertility advice for the site. |
| Mawese / oil palm | Bearing stage | Low-quality seedlings and poor management reduce oil yield and oil quality. | Use improved, verified hybrid seedlings. TARI Kihinga also reports work on illegitimacy screening of hybrid oil palm seedlings to reduce contamination in crossing programs. |
| Mawese / oil palm | Bearing stage | Pests and diseases can reduce bunch yield and quality. | Use healthy planting material, monitor the plantation, and follow TARI / extension recommendations for plant protection. |
| Mawese / oil palm | Harvesting | Harvesting unripe bunches reduces oil quality and oil extraction efficiency. | Harvest ripe bunches and process them promptly to protect quality. |
| Mawese / oil palm | Post-harvest / value chain | Poor post-harvest handling reduces the value of the oil. | Keep harvested fruit / bunches moving quickly into clean processing and value-addition steps. |

## Official source basis used for this draft

- **Ministry of Agriculture / TARI:** *Mwongozo wa Uzalishaji Mazao* (official ecological crop guide for Tanzania).
- **TARI:** *Kilimo Bora Cha Mahindi*.
- **TARI:** bean disease / pest and bean variety materials, including common bean disease research outputs.
- **TARI Naliendele:** groundnut / karanga mandate and variety catalogue materials.
- **TARI Kihinga:** michikichi / oil palm research and quality-planting-material statements.
- **TARI:** *Uandaaji na Uoteshaji wa Mbegu ya Michikichi*.
